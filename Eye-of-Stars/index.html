Eye of Stars

Eyes of Stars

At night,
I looked up into sky,
The bright eyes of the stars,
come into my eye.

It’s night, they
See beings as busy as ants,
See the lights from houses, smoke from chimneys.
They overlooked the Earth,
Silently a hundred years had passed.
Stars, observers of the Earth,
They are the audience that never leaves the stage.

They may have seen:
Two rebels are plotting a coup at night,
Socrates sighs at sunset with panic,
Wang Yangming* drinks with the world in the desert mountain,
alone and lonely.
And failed explorers face their own fate.

Stars have no tears,
Stars sigh not,
They’re just faithfully receiving this light.
No ifs, no hows,
This world is but a speck of dust among the stars.

Anything you love, Anything you hate,
The things you won’t forget, the things you would cherish,
Never stepped out of this little dust.
In the vast sea of stars,
There are probably thousands of other such worlds.
Our story, to stars,
Just repeating what’s been repeated a million times,
Joys and sorrows, rain or shine.

Stars are watching, and time is fleeting.
A flash of fire on the Sumerian plain,
Caught by a pious hand on the side of the Nile.
The great wave washes the sand, a teenager in the Yellow River side is holding up a just-made clay idol.
The world is changing so fast,
The nation of voyagers has set its sails
White steam spurted from the steel, ringing the busy music.
Some strange machines, like birds, boldly conquer the territory of the wind.

A dream lasts a thousand years.
We hold in hand our dust that we now live on,
Seeming to forget the sea of stars.
But stars never forget, and they are onlookers forever.
Even though a thousand years have passed stars are as before.
Stars are eternal, stars are silent.

What have you seen these years, Stars?
Dynasty changes, starvation all over, or a whole society revolution?
Still don’t touch your nerve, do you?
This speck of dust means nothing to you,
But for us,
It is the whole world.
We are born here, a spot in your eyes,
A particle of no importance,
A point joins all the joys and sorrows of the human race.
It is our world.

Stars see all,
And say nothing.
Kings and nobles, heroes and heroines,
They do not care at all.
The light of our world traverses the void,
Will reach the unimaginable distance.
In the silent black exhibition hall,
The Earth is the only exhibit.

At the end of it all,
Stars are eternal eyes,
Faithful and cold.
The world is small, fragile and short,
But the stars are not partial or indifferent.
Under the gaze of stars,
The human story will continue,
Maybe one day they will leave this land.

At night,
I looked up into sky,
The bright eyes of the stars,
Caught my eye…

Note: Wang Yang-ming (王陽明, Japanese Ō Yōmei, 1472–1529) was a Ming Dynasty Chinese idealist Neo-Confucian scholar–official. After Zhu Xi, he is commonly considered the most important Neo-Confucian thinker, with an interpretation of Confucianism that denied the rationalist dualism found in the orthodox philosophy of Zhu Xi. He was the leading figure in the Neo-Confucian School of Mind, which championed an interpretation of Mencius (a Classical Confucian) that unified knowledge and action. Due to the depth of his scholarship and his scintillating style, he was known as Yang-ming Xiansheng (Brilliant Master Yang) in literary circles.
